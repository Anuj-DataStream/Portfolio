01 / ORIGIN

The Challenge Origin

How a broken lab project became a personal mission

In mid-November 2023, our science teacher Jyoti Bhardwaj informed the class that a zonal level science exhibition was approaching. Initially, only two classmates — Anmol and Yashika — were officially selected. They were assigned an inherited senior project stored inside the RA Lab.

When they saw the actual condition of the model, they quickly realized that none of them understood Arduino circuits, sensors, or how the prototype was originally supposed to function. Since I was already known for my strong interest in technology experiments and hardware curiosity, they approached me for technical help. What started as one lab visit gradually turned into full technical ownership — because there was no one else who could understand or repair it in the given time.

02 / ANALYSIS

Rebuild Decision Timeline

Four phases from dead hardware to exhibition-ready

PHASE 01 // INSPECTION
Condition Assessment
The inherited model was almost entirely non-functional. Most jumper wires were detached, several light connections were dead, the Arduino-based road blocker system was not responding, and the integrated transportation modules had no clear labeling or wiring map. Even the Avishkaar BLE robotic unit stored with the project was not properly arranged.
NON-FUNCTIONAL INHERITED UNIT
PHASE 02 // DECISION
Full Rebuild from Scratch
My first instinct was to try basic repair and reconnect broken wires, but after observing repeated failures I realized that random patchwork would not make the project stable. With no documentation and no senior available for explanation, I took the decision to dismantle the dead understanding completely, study every subsystem separately, and rebuild the working logic from scratch.
CRITICAL DECISION POINT
PHASE 03 // LEARNING
Rapid Self-Education (6–7 hrs/day)
School hours were spent in the RA Lab physically inspecting the dead board, while evenings at home were spent watching YouTube tutorials, searching for similar sensor combinations, collecting sample code snippets, and performing hit-and-trial breadboard experiments. I separately tested LED blinking, servo rotation, ultrasonic object detection, and buzzer activation until I understood each component before reconnecting them into the main model.
~40 HRS INTENSIVE LEARNING
PHASE 04 // EXECUTION
Final Stabilization & Exhibition
The final board was transported to the host school in a partially stabilized condition. Final preparation time was spent in continuous troubleshooting — reconnecting wires, retesting the buzzer, aligning the servo barrier, and making the board presentable before judges arrived.
3RD POSITION SECURED
03 / ENGINEERING

Subsystem Breakdown

Five restored modules powering the CARONYCLEANSER ecosystem

MODULE 01

Automated Road Blocker

An ultrasonic sensor detected incoming vehicles and triggered an Arduino-controlled servo barrier that rotated upward to block unsafe movement. This was the most difficult module — both circuit connections and Arduino logic were unstable and required repeated testing.

Arduino Uno Ultrasonic HC-SR04 Servo Motor
MODULE 02

Zebra Crossing Alert

An IR sensor beneath the zebra crossing detected object presence and activated a buzzer alert for approaching traffic. This pedestrian awareness mechanism frequently failed during early tests because of loose battery and sensor alignment issues.

IR Sensor Buzzer Active Alert
MODULE 03

Automatic Street Lights

Blue LED street lights illuminated automatically when ambient light dropped using an LDR sensing arrangement. Multiple LED joints had to be resoldered and rewired to make the roadway lighting look synchronized across the full model.

LDR Sensor LED Array Resolder
MODULE 04

Hydraulic Parking System

A syringe-water pressure hydraulic lift demonstrated vertical parking optimization using a manually actuated single-platform system. Though simple mechanically, it improved the visual appeal of the board by adding another transportation concept.

Hydraulics Pressure Lift Manual Actuator
MODULE 05

Avishkaar BLE Robot

A four-wheel Avishkaar 2.0 BLE robotic mobility unit controlled via mobile phone strengthened the overall technology impact. Reassembled and tested separately so that the final display had an additional moving robotics element during demonstration.

BLE Brain 2.0 Mobile Control 4-Wheel Drive

04 / TEAM

Role Distribution

An honest account of who carried the technical load

Although the official science exhibition team consisted of Anmol, Yashika, and me, the actual technical workload distribution was highly uneven. Approximately 85–90% of the technical understanding, debugging, rewiring, code arrangement, subsystem restoration, and final troubleshooting was handled by me. Anmol and Yashika supported by bringing materials, helping in poster/file preparation, learning the explanation points, and doing the final decorative arrangement when required. During the last stage, 3–4 more classmates briefly joined only for decoration support, but the engineering execution remained concentrated around the core technical restoration process I was leading.

05 / EXHIBITION

Exhibition Day: Pressure & Recovery

29 November 2023 — the most critical day of execution

The final board was transported to the host school laboratory in a partially stabilized condition. Decoration remained incomplete, multiple modules were still unstable, and nearby competitors openly mocked the unfinished appearance of the project during setup, which significantly reduced our confidence.

Instead of entering the venue with celebration, we entered with the fear that the project might fail publicly. Final preparation time was spent in continuous troubleshooting — reconnecting wires, retesting the buzzer, aligning the servo barrier, making the robot ready, and trying to make the board look presentable before judges arrived.
Although Anmol and Yashika handled most front-facing explanation, I remained the technical fallback throughout the evaluation whenever a module needed urgent correction or judges asked for deeper understanding of the electronics and sensor logic.

This was a defining test of composure — operating under public scrutiny, competitor mockery, and the constant threat of live module failure, while still delivering a technically sound demonstration that impressed the judging panel enough to secure second position.

06 / RESULT

The Result

Against all odds — and our own expectations

🏆
2nd

Position — Zonal Level

Against our own expectations, the project secured Second Position at the Zonal Level Science Exhibition. The result itself was shocking because until the final hours we were simply trying not to embarrass ourselves with a broken setup.

⚠ RECOGNITION GAP: Certificates issued to officially registered members only
The emotional turning point came during the award ceremony: since only Anmol and Yashika had been officially registered before my involvement, they were called on stage to receive certificates and the formal trophy. I remained without official recognition despite being the major technical contributor.

This became one of the strongest professional lessons of my early journey — technical contribution alone is not enough; work must also be documented, represented, and visibly claimed if one wants rightful ownership in collaborative environments.

07 / TAKEAWAYS

Long-Term Engineering Lessons

What this project permanently changed about my engineering mindset

01
⚡
Rapid Learning Under Pressure Learn rapidly when responsibility becomes unavoidable. The best engineering education often comes when there is no fallback — forced ownership accelerates skill acquisition like nothing else.
02
🔁
Reverse Engineering > Passive Theory Reverse engineering teaches more than passive theory because failure forces practical understanding. Dismantling a broken system reveals architecture that no tutorial fully explains.
03
🐛
Debugging is Built Through Failure Debugging is built through repeated failure, not one-shot success. Every failed reconnection, every unstable module, and every test loop became the foundation of actual embedded systems intuition.
04
📋
Document & Claim Your Work Technical contribution must be documented for professional ownership and visible credit. If the work is invisible, so is the contributor — no matter how much of the load was carried.
